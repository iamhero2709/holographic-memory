\begin{abstract}
Knowledge graph embedding (KGE) models such as TransE, DistMult, ComplEx and
RotatE achieve strong single-hop link prediction but are not designed to answer
\emph{zero-shot compositional} queries: multi-hop questions whose relation chain
was never observed during training. Holographic Reduced Representations (HRR),
which bind and unbind symbols through circular convolution, are a theoretically
appealing candidate for such reasoning because binding is approximately
invertible and composable. We conduct a controlled study of two holographic
memory variants---real-valued HRR and phase-only Fourier HRR (FHRR)---each paired
with a modern Hopfield cleanup, on the full FB15k-237 test set over five random
seeds. We report three findings. First, both variants are competitive
\emph{atomic} retrievers in the ranking sense: real HRR attains a filtered MRR of
$0.358 \pm 0.002$ and FHRR $0.350 \pm 0.021$, in the range of standard baselines
(we stress this is not a parameter-matched comparison). Second, despite this,
\emph{both} variants fail at two-hop zero-shot composition: a one-sided binomial
test cannot reject the hypothesis that their accuracy equals chance
($p = 0.22$ and $p = 0.85$ respectively), and accuracy is flat across all cleanup
temperatures. Third, we characterise the FHRR failure mechanism and find it is not
the magnitude (``modulus collapse'') story sometimes assumed for phasor memories:
the cleanup re-projects onto the unit torus, so $|z| = 1$ by construction. Two
independent probes instead show the cleaned representation carries no phase
information about the target---phasor cosine of $-0.009 \pm 0.006$ to the true
entity versus $0.005 \pm 0.003$ to a random one, and a mean absolute phase error
of $1.59$\,rad at the final hop, indistinguishable from the uniform value $\pi/2$.
We resolve an apparent paradox---atomic ranking survives this near-random
phase because it integrates aggregate codebook similarity, whereas the second
bind is a per-component phase operation the cleanup does not support. An untrained
control confirms training buys atomic competence but never composition. The
limitation is architectural, and the cure must preserve phase coherence, not
merely magnitude, across hops.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Knowledge Graph Embeddings, Holographic Reduced
Representations, Vector Symbolic Architectures, Compositional Reasoning, Modern
Hopfield Networks, Zero-Shot Generalisation.

% ============================================================
\section{Introduction}
% ============================================================

Knowledge graphs (KGs) store facts as triples $(h, r, t)$, e.g.\
\emph{(Marie\_Curie, nationality, Poland)}. Knowledge graph embedding (KGE)
methods learn vector representations of entities and relations so that plausible
triples receive high scores~\cite{bordes2013transe, yang2014distmult,
trouillon2016complex, sun2019rotate}. They are trained on, and evaluated against,
\emph{single-hop} queries: ``given $h$ and $r$, predict $t$''.

Many questions of interest are inherently \emph{compositional}: they chain
several relations never jointly observed. ``Which administrative regions lie in
the country a given person is a national of?'' requires composing
\emph{nationality} with \emph{contains}. If that composite never appears in
training, a standard KGE model has no mechanism to infer it. We call this setting
\emph{zero-shot compositional reasoning}.

\paragraph{Why holographic memory?}
Holographic Reduced Representations (HRR)~\cite{plate2003holographic} and the
broader family of Vector Symbolic Architectures
(VSA)~\cite{kanerva2009hyperdimensional, schlegel2022comparison} define an algebra
over distributed vectors in which symbols are \emph{bound} together and later
\emph{unbound}. The binding operator---circular convolution---is approximately
invertible and associative, suggesting a memory storing facts as bound bundles
could answer multi-hop queries by composing unbinding operations. Pairing this
algebra with a modern Hopfield network~\cite{ramsauer2021hopfield} as an
associative ``cleanup'' yields a natural candidate for zero-shot composition. Our
central question is empirical: \emph{does this candidate work, and if not, why?}

\paragraph{Contributions.}
All claims below are verified over five random seeds on the full FB15k-237 test
set under a leakage-controlled protocol.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.4em, itemsep=2pt]
    \item \textbf{Both holographic variants are competitive atomic retrievers}
    (in the ranking sense). Real HRR reaches filtered MRR $0.358 \pm 0.002$ and
    FHRR $0.350 \pm 0.021$, in the range of standard single-hop baselines
    (Section~\ref{sec:results-atomic}). An ablation shows the Hopfield cleanup
    accounts for about half of the achieved accuracy. These are not
    parameter-matched comparisons.

    \item \textbf{Both variants fail at zero-shot composition.} A one-sided
    binomial test against chance cannot be rejected for either model
    ($p = 0.22$, $p = 0.85$), and zero-shot accuracy is flat across all cleanup
    temperatures (Section~\ref{sec:results-zeroshot}). This is a negative result,
    reported plainly.

    \item \textbf{We characterise the FHRR failure mechanism.} The
    magnitude-based ``modulus collapse'' intuition does not apply once the cleanup
    re-projects onto the unit torus ($|z| = 1$). Two phase probes show the cleaned
    representation carries no directional information about the target, and we
    resolve the resulting paradox---atomic ranking survives near-random phase
    because it integrates aggregate similarity, whereas the second bind needs
    per-component phase coherence the cleanup does not supply
    (Section~\ref{sec:results-mechanism}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Knowledge graph embeddings.}
TransE~\cite{bordes2013transe} models a relation as a translation
$h + r \approx t$. DistMult~\cite{yang2014distmult} uses a bilinear diagonal
score; ComplEx~\cite{trouillon2016complex} extends it to complex embeddings for
asymmetry; RotatE~\cite{sun2019rotate} represents relations as rotations,
$t \approx h \circ r$. All are strong single-hop predictors with no native
mechanism for composing unseen relation chains.

\paragraph{Compositional and path-based reasoning.}
The path-query framework of Guu et al.~\cite{guu2015traversing} composes relation
embeddings along a path in vector space; NeuralLP~\cite{yang2017neurallp} and
RNNLogic~\cite{qu2021rnnlogic} learn logical rules. These typically require
supervision on paths or rules. We study the stricter zero-shot setting in which
the composed relation is withheld entirely, and we do not claim to outperform
trained compositional methods; we ask whether holographic binding \emph{alone}
composes.

\paragraph{Holographic and vector-symbolic memory.}
HRR~\cite{plate2003holographic} introduced circular convolution as a binding
operator over real vectors; hyperdimensional
computing~\cite{kanerva2009hyperdimensional} and VSA
surveys~\cite{schlegel2022comparison} study related algebras, including the
Fourier HRR (FHRR) variant on unit-modulus phasors with element-wise complex
multiplication. The VSA literature discusses noise accumulation under repeated
binding in general terms; here we measure, for an FHRR-with-Hopfield-cleanup
pipeline specifically, \emph{which} quantity (magnitude vs.\ phase) is lost.

\paragraph{Modern Hopfield networks.}
Ramsauer et al.~\cite{ramsauer2021hopfield} showed the update rule of continuous
modern Hopfield networks coincides with attention and retrieves stored patterns
via a softmax over similarities. We use this as the cleanup that maps a noisy
unbound estimate back to a codebook entry.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}
% ============================================================

Let $E$ be a set of entities and $R$ a set of relations; a knowledge graph is a
triple set $T \subseteq E \times R \times E$, split into disjoint
$T_{\text{tr}}, T_{\text{va}}, T_{\text{te}}$.

\paragraph{Single-hop (atomic) task.}
Given $(h, r)$, predict $t$ with $(h, r, t) \in T_{\text{te}}$. We rank all
candidate tails and report filtered metrics.

\paragraph{Two-hop compositional task.}
For a chain $(r_1, r_2)$, the compositional answer set for head $h$ is
\begin{equation}
A_{h, r_1, r_2}
= \{\, t \in E \;:\; \exists\, m \in E,\;
(h, r_1, m) \in T \;\wedge\; (m, r_2, t) \in T \,\}.
\end{equation}
The model predicts an element of $A_{h, r_1, r_2}$ without having been trained on
the composite $r_1 \circ r_2$ and without an explicit representation of the
intermediate entity $m$.

\paragraph{Zero-shot protocol and leakage control.}
We extract the ten most frequent two-hop chains in FB15k-237 (Algorithm~\ref{alg:split}).
``Train support'' for a chain $(r_1, r_2)$ is the number of distinct mid-entities
$m$ such that some $(h, r_1, m)$ and $(m, r_2, t)$ both occur in training; we
require it to be $\geq 100$. The individual relations $r_1, r_2$ are themselves
seen atomically in training; only their \emph{composition} is withheld. Chains
with a repeated relation ($r_1 = r_2$) are permitted and handled identically,
since the intermediate-entity constraint in $A_{h,r_1,r_2}$ is defined per hop and
does not assume $r_1 \neq r_2$. To avoid trivial answers we remove every $(h, t)$ for which $t$ is directly reachable from
$h$ via any single training relation. This removes $98$ pairs ($0.14\%$), leaving
$69{,}855$ genuinely zero-shot pairs. Chance accuracy is
$1/|E| \approx 6.8 \times 10^{-5}$.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Leakage-controlled zero-shot pair construction}
\label{alg:split}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{Input:} train graph $G_{\text{tr}}$, test graph $G_{\text{te}}$, number of chains $n$
\State For each ordered $(r_1,r_2)$, count chain train support: distinct mid-entities linking $r_1$ then $r_2$ in $G_{\text{tr}}$
\State In $G_{\text{te}}$, collect head--tail pairs reachable by each $(r_1,r_2)$
\State Keep the $n$ chains with most test pairs subject to chain train support $\geq 100$
\For{each kept chain $(r_1,r_2)$ and test pair $(h,t)$}
    \If{$t$ is a direct train neighbour of $h$ under any relation}
        \State discard $(h,t)$ \Comment{leakage}
    \Else
        \State add $(h, r_1, r_2, t)$ to the zero-shot set
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Holographic algebra}
For $a, b \in \Rb^D$, circular convolution binds them,
\begin{equation}
(a \bind b)_k = \sum_{j=0}^{D-1} a_j\, b_{(k-j) \bmod D},
\qquad
\widehat{a \bind b} = \hat{a} \odot \hat{b},
\end{equation}
with $\hat{\cdot}$ the DFT and $\odot$ element-wise product ($O(D \log D)$ via the
FFT). Unbinding uses the conjugate,
$a \approx (a \bind b) \unbind b = \mathcal{F}^{-1}( \widehat{a \bind b} \odot
\overline{\hat{b}})$. Facts are superposed into one memory vector,
$M = \sum_{(h, r, t) \in T_{\text{tr}}} e_h \bind \rho_r \bind e_t$, with entity
embeddings $e_x$ and role embeddings $\rho_r$.

\subsection{Hopfield cleanup}
Unbinding from a superposed memory is noisy. A modern Hopfield cleanup maps the
estimate back to the entity codebook $E \in \Rb^{|E| \times D}$,
\begin{equation}
\cleanupop(z) = \softmaxop\!\big(\beta\, \langle z, E \rangle\big)\, E,
\label{eq:cleanup}
\end{equation}
with inverse temperature $\beta$; a hard variant uses straight-through
$\argmaxop$.

\subsection{Real HRR and Complex FHRR}
\textbf{Real HRR} uses real embeddings $e_x, \rho_r \in \Rb^{D}$, $D{=}1024$.
Atomic prediction is $\hat{t} = \cleanupop( M \unbind (e_h \bind \rho_r))$ scored
against $E$; the two-hop prediction unbinds twice, cleaning the intermediate
(hard) before the second bind. \textbf{Complex FHRR} represents each symbol by
phases $\phi \in [-\pi, \pi]^{D}$, $D{=}512$, as a unit phasor $z = e^{i\phi}$.
Binding is element-wise complex multiplication (phase addition); unbinding
multiplies by the conjugate. Because FHRR embeddings are complex, $D{=}512$
complex coordinates equal $1024$ real numbers---the same real parameter budget as
Real HRR, so the two models are roughly parameter-matched to each other. Crucially,
the FHRR cleanup extracts the phase of the convex combination and re-embeds it as
$e^{i\phi}$, \emph{forcing $|z|=1$ at every cleanup output}---central to
Section~\ref{sec:results-mechanism}. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} shows both
flows.

\subsection{Training objective}
Both models minimise a cross-entropy atomic loss plus a contrastive invertibility
regulariser $\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{atom}} + \lambda\,
\mathcal{L}_{\text{inv}}$, $\lambda{=}0.2$, where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{inv}}$
encourages $f \bind r$ to unbind back to $f$ and $r$. The memory $M$ is Hebbian
initialised and refined by gradient descent. We use Adam (lr $10^{-3}$,
cosine-annealed to $10^{-5}$), batch $2048$, gradient clip $1.0$.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
% ============================================================
\paragraph{Dataset.}
Canonical FB15k-237: $272{,}115$ train, $17{,}535$ valid, $20{,}466$ test
triples; $|E| = 14{,}778$ entities (entities and relations share one index space)
and $237$ relations.

\paragraph{Metrics.}
\emph{Top-1 accuracy} is the fraction of test queries whose top-scored candidate
is the gold tail. We also report filtered \emph{MRR} and \emph{Hits@}$\{1,3,10\}$,
removing all other true tails before scoring the gold tail. Crucially, all
ranking metrics are computed on the \emph{full} test set ($20{,}466$ queries), not
a sample. For composition we report top-1 zero-shot accuracy over the pairs of
Algorithm~\ref{alg:split} and a one-sided binomial test against chance.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.}
All headline numbers are mean $\pm$ std over seeds $\{1,2,3,4,42\}$. Real HRR uses
$D{=}1024$, $\beta{=}8$; FHRR $D{=}512$ complex ($=1024$ real), $\beta{=}12$.

\subsection{Both variants are competitive atomic retrievers}
\label{sec:results-atomic}

\paragraph{The Hopfield cleanup carries half the performance.}
Replacing the cleanup of Eq.~\eqref{eq:cleanup} with a direct normalisation of the
unbound estimate (\emph{no Hopfield}) drops real-HRR atomic accuracy from $0.158$
to $0.081$---a relative decrease of $\approx 48\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:ablation-core}).
The cleanup is essential, not cosmetic. We do not ablate the FHRR cleanup
separately because its phase re-projection is architecturally mandatory: without
it the representation leaves the unit torus and binding is no longer well defined.

\subsection{Both variants fail at zero-shot composition}
\label{sec:results-zeroshot}

In sharp contrast to atomic performance, two-hop zero-shot accuracy is at or near
chance for \emph{both} variants. Mean accuracy is $1.7\times 10^{-4}$ (real HRR)
and $2.9\times 10^{-5}$ (FHRR), against chance $6.8\times 10^{-5}$. We run a
one-sided binomial test per seed ($H_0$: accuracy $=$ chance). For FHRR the null
is not rejected at $\alpha = 0.05$ for any of the five seeds (per-seed $p$ in
$[0.34, 1.0]$): there is no evidence FHRR exceeds chance. For real HRR the picture
is marginal---the null is rejected for three of five seeds (per-seed $p$ in
$[0.0000, 0.99]$)---but the absolute accuracy remains tiny ($\sim\!1.7\times
10^{-4}$, i.e.\ a handful of the $69{,}855$ pairs), so even where it is
statistically above chance it is far from any usable signal and orders of
magnitude below atomic performance. We therefore describe both models as failing
the compositional task. The failure is not a temperature artifact---sweeping
$\beta \in \{1,5,10,20,50\}$ leaves accuracy flat and near chance
(Figure~\ref{fig:beta}). No temperature elicits composition, indicating a
structural obstacle.

\subsection{The FHRR failure mechanism: phase, not magnitude}
\label{sec:results-mechanism}

A common intuition for phasor memories under iterated binding is \emph{modulus
collapse}: the softmax cleanup of Eq.~\eqref{eq:cleanup} is a convex combination
of unit phasors, so its output should have $|z| \ll 1$, decaying toward a noise
floor over hops. We tested this directly and found it inapplicable here.

\paragraph{The modulus probe is uninformative by construction.}
The mean per-component modulus of the cleaned representation is $|z| = 1.0000$
(std $0.0000$, min $=$ max $= 1.0000$) at \emph{both} hops, across all five seeds.
The reason is architectural: the FHRR cleanup extracts the phase of the convex
combination and re-embeds it as a unit phasor $e^{i\phi}$, so $|z| = 1$ \emph{by
construction}. Any magnitude-based diagnostic is therefore vacuous for this model;
the modulus-collapse narrative does not apply once cleanup re-normalises onto the
unit torus.

\paragraph{Probe 1 --- phase coherence.}
Since magnitude is fixed, only the phase can carry information. We measure the
phasor cosine similarity between the cleaned two-hop representation and (i) the
\emph{true} target and (ii) a \emph{random} entity (Figure~\ref{fig:coherence}).
Across five seeds, similarity to the true target is $-0.009 \pm 0.006$---
statistically indistinguishable from, and on average slightly below, the
$0.005 \pm 0.003$ to a random entity. Both are effectively zero: the cleaned
representation retains \emph{no} directional information about the answer.

We measure the per-component absolute phase error between the cleaned

note that the hop-1 measurement requires a ground-truth intermediate entity; when
a head reaches several mid-entities via $r_1$ we use the first one that also
completes the chain to the gold target, so the hop-1 number carries mild ambiguity

\paragraph{Probes 3--4 --- renormalisation and hard cleanup do not help.}
Two interventions that would rescue a \emph{magnitude}-driven failure have no
effect on a \emph{phase}-driven one. Explicitly re-normalising intermediate and
final representations to unit modulus leaves zero-shot accuracy at $0.000000$;
replacing the soft cleanup with a hard straight-through $\argmaxop$ at both hops
likewise yields $0.000000$. Neither magnitude restoration nor sharper selection
recovers the lost phase.

\paragraph{The paradox: atomic ranking survives near-random phase.}
A puzzle remains: how can FHRR achieve MRR $0.350$ when even the first-hop cleaned
phase is near uniform? The resolution is that the two tasks use different signals.
We measured the atomic softmax \emph{margin} (gold logit minus the largest
incorrect logit). It is negative on average ($-1.82$ for FHRR, $-1.92$ for real
HRR), and the gold entity is the single top choice only $\approx 13$--$16\%$ of
the time---consistent with top-1 accuracy. Yet MRR is high ($0.35$), so the gold
entity is reliably ranked among the top few even when not first. In other words,
atomic competence is a \emph{ranking} property recovered by integrating aggregate
codebook similarity across all $D$ dimensions, robust to per-component phase
noise. Composition, by contrast, feeds the cleaned intermediate back into a
\emph{bind}, a per-component phase operation: it requires each component's phase to
be correct, not merely the aggregate similarity to be peaked. The cleanup supplies
the latter but not the former, so single-hop ranking survives while the second
bind operates on phase noise.

\paragraph{An untrained control isolates training from architecture.}
A freshly initialised FHRR has atomic accuracy $\approx 0$ while the trained model
reaches $0.126 \pm 0.001$---training clearly helps single-hop retrieval. Yet both
are equally at chance on the two-hop task. Optimisation buys atomic competence but
never composition; the limitation is a property of the architecture, not of the
training run.

% ============================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\paragraph{Why the phase is lost.}
Each unbinding from the superposed memory $M$ injects cross-talk from every other
stored fact. The Hopfield cleanup is strong enough to rank the correct entity from
this noisy estimate---hence atomic retrieval works in the ranking sense---but the
phasor it emits is reconstructed from a softmax-weighted average whose
per-component phase is close to uniform. Binding is a per-component phase
operation, so feeding this phase-incoherent intermediate into the second bind
compounds noise rather than signal, and the second unbinding adds a fresh layer of
cross-talk. By the final cleanup the angular signal that should point at the
target has been averaged away. The unit-modulus constraint that makes FHRR elegant
for a single binding is precisely what removes the magnitude headroom that might
otherwise have flagged low confidence, leaving a confident-looking but
directionless phasor.

\paragraph{Real HRR fails too.}
Real HRR also fails at two-hop composition. We did not isolate a single clean
mechanism comparable to the FHRR phase analysis; the likeliest explanation is the
same accumulation of memory cross-talk across hops, here as additive noise rather
than phase mixing. We avoid over-attributing the small atomic gap between the
models, since they also differ in representation; a full mechanistic account of
real-HRR composition failure is left open.

\paragraph{Implications.}
Our findings caution against two intuitions. First, competitive single-hop
performance of a holographic memory does \emph{not} imply any capacity for
zero-shot composition; the two are decoupled here. Second, the ``modulus
collapse'' picture is not universal: once a cleanup re-normalises onto the unit
torus, magnitude is fixed and the relevant failure is in the phase. Architectures
intending to compose with phasor memories will need mechanisms that explicitly
preserve phase coherence across hops---e.g.\ per-hop error correction,
confidence-aware cleanup that retains magnitude, or memory structures that reduce
inter-fact cross-talk.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
This study is deliberately controlled and therefore narrow.
(i)~We evaluate on a single real dataset (FB15k-237); behaviour on sparser graphs
(WN18RR) or small ontologies (UMLS) may differ and is future work.
(ii)~The baseline figures in Table~\ref{tab:atomic} are representative literature
values, not a controlled re-implementation; the atomic comparison is indicative
only, and we do not claim to beat any baseline.
(iii)~We do not compare against trained compositional methods
(e.g.\ Guu et al.~\cite{guu2015traversing}); our question is whether holographic
binding \emph{alone} composes, and the answer is negative.
(iv)~We study two-hop chains over ten high-frequency relation pairs; deeper or more
diverse compositions, and a breakdown by relation cardinality (1-1/1-N/N-N), are
untested.
(v)~The hop-1 phase-error measurement uses an approximate ground-truth
intermediate when a head reaches multiple mid-entities; the hop-2 measurement,
which drives our conclusion, does not. None of these caveats affects the central
result---chance-level composition with a phase-localised mechanism for FHRR---but
they bound its scope.

% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================
We studied holographic memory with a modern Hopfield cleanup as a candidate for
zero-shot compositional reasoning in knowledge graphs. Both real and Fourier
variants proved competitive single-hop retrievers in the ranking sense (filtered
MRR $0.358$ and $0.350$), with the Hopfield cleanup responsible for about half of
the achieved accuracy. Yet both failed at two-hop zero-shot composition, at
statistically chance accuracy and independent of cleanup temperature. Our main
contribution is mechanistic: the commonly assumed modulus-collapse explanation is
inapplicable to a cleanup that re-normalises onto the unit torus, and \emph{phase
decorrelation} is the operative failure mode for FHRR---the cleaned two-hop
representation carries no more target information than a random entity, with phase
error at the uniform $\pi/2$ limit. We resolve the apparent paradox that atomic
ranking survives this near-random phase: single-hop readout integrates aggregate
similarity, whereas the second bind demands per-component phase coherence the
cleanup does not provide. We hope this sharpens the target for future
compositional reasoners: preserve phase coherence, not merely magnitude, across
hops.
